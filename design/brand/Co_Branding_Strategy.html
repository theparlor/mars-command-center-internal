1. The Strategic "Why"

Every logo on a slide costs content real estate. The decision to consume that space must be intentional — it has to earn its keep. This isn't decoration; it's a deliberate expression of the consulting relationship.

For Subaru: Ownership & Investment

Seeing their brand dominant on materials about their transformation signals: "This is YOUR program, not something being done to you." Dominant brand presence builds psychological ownership at the executive level. When Marc or Natalie shows a deck to their leadership, Subaru's identity frames the narrative — it's an internal initiative that happens to have expert support, not an outside vendor's project.

For Turnberry: IP Attribution & Referenceability

Subtle but unmistakable presence establishes intellectual property attribution. These decks will travel — status updates get forwarded, training materials get archived, frameworks get referenced months later. When a Subaru leader moves to another company (and they will), Turnberry's name is embedded in the body of work they remember. This is how consulting firms build referenceability without competing for attention.

The Consulting-Firm Convention

McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte, and Accenture all follow the same pattern: client brand dominant, firm brand present but subordinate. This isn't humility — it's strategic. The consulting firm's value is demonstrated by the quality of the work, not the size of their logo. The logo just makes sure the attribution sticks.

2. The Height Budget

The most common objection to co-branding is "it eats into content space." Here's why that doesn't apply when done correctly:

Content Zone3.55"63%Sacred space. No logos, ever. This is where the work lives.

The Key Insight

Logos consume width within existing structural bands, not additional height. The hero band and footer already exist in every build script. We're filling space that was previously underutilized — the right side of the hero band held only a date badge, and the footer had room for both a text attribution and a small logo mark. The net vertical cost of co-branding is zero inches.

4. Information Design Principles

F-Pattern Eye Tracking

Users scan slides in an F-pattern: top-left first, then across, then down. Subaru gets the top-left because that's where the eye lands first. This isn't arbitrary — it's where the brain assigns "owner" status to the content that follows.

Attribution Position (Lower-Right)

The lower-right corner is where readers expect to find "who made this." Book publishers, news organizations, and consulting firms all place attribution marks here. It's the "signature line" of visual communication.

Progressive Disclosure of Partnership

Title slides show both brands clearly. Interior slides whisper the relationship. Section dividers use the Subaru crest. The closing slide makes the partnership explicit. This arc mirrors how trust develops — it doesn't shout at the start.

Text vs. Image Hierarchy

A logo image always commands more attention than text. By using Subaru's logo as an image and Turnberry's name as text on interior slides, we create a natural visual hierarchy without explicitly declaring one brand "more important." The medium itself communicates the relationship.

5. Anti-Patterns

Common co-branding mistakes that erode professionalism and waste real estate:

Dueling Logos — Two logo images side-by-side in every slide footer. Creates visual competition in a zone that should be quiet. One image + one text = clean.
Logo Soup — More than two logos on any single slide. If there's a third party (e.g., Atlassian for Jira), use text attribution only.
Equal Sizing — Making both logos the same size signals ambiguity about whose program this is. Subaru should always be larger — it's their transformation.
Logo as Decoration — Scattering logos on slides that don't need them. Data slides, tables, and charts earn 100% of their real estate for content.
Footer Logo Wars — Both logos as images in the footer, fighting for 0.5" of vertical space. This is the single most common co-branding failure in enterprise consulting.
